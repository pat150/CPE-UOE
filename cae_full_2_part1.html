Examiner Rationale
1 A — burst
✅ Correct
"burst onto the scene" — unambiguous fixed expression conveying sudden, dramatic arrival
Strong distractor
emerged — dramatic register but not a fixed phrase; "emerged onto the scene" is unnatural
Weak distractor
came — too vague and informal; "came onto the scene" lacks idiomatic force
Obvious distractor
arrived — too literal; implies physical location, not sudden prominence
2 C — driven
✅ Correct
"driven leadership" — precise collocation implying intense internal motivation and ambition
Strong distractor
forceful — tempting, implies strong leadership, but suggests aggression rather than motivation
Weak distractor
stubborn — related to determination but carries clearly negative connotation
Obvious distractor
lively — too trivial and informal; entirely wrong register for leadership description
3 D — clinching
✅ Correct
"clinch a title" — precise sporting collocation for winning a closely contested competition
Strong distractor
grabbing — common informal sporting language but too colloquial for C1 written register
Weak distractor
achieving — related but "achieving a title" is not a natural sporting collocation
Obvious distractor
obtaining — formal register but entirely wrong for a sporting context; eliminable quickly
4 A — jubilation
✅ Correct
"jubilation" — denotes mass public outpouring of joy, precisely right for a historic 81-year wait
Strong distractor
elation — high-register synonym but describes personal emotion, not collective public reaction
Weak distractor
celebration — too general; does not convey the intensity or historic significance
Obvious distractor
excitement — far too mild for scenes marking an 81-year wait; register mismatch
5 B — followed suit
✅ Correct
"followed suit" — fixed expression meaning to do the same as another party; unambiguous
Strong distractor
caught on — tempting phrasal verb but means "became popular", entirely different meaning
Weak distractor
kept pace — means maintained the same speed; wrong meaning in context
Obvious distractor
fell in line — means complied with authority; clearly wrong in a sporting achievement context
6 C — overhauled
✅ Correct
"overhaul" — implies radical, comprehensive reform of an entire system; stronger than alternatives
Strong distractor
restructured — implies organisational change rather than rule or format reform
Weak distractor
reformed — suggests only partial or moral change; lacks the scale of "overhaul"
Obvious distractor
revised — too minor in scale; associated with text editing rather than systemic change
7 D — soared
✅ Correct
"soared" — conveys dramatic, rapid increase; precisely right for attendances in a golden era
Strong distractor
climbed — upward movement but implies gradual effort rather than dramatic surge
Weak distractor
rose — too gradual and neutral; no sense of scale or speed
Obvious distractor
increased — completely neutral; conveys no emotional or dramatic weight whatsoever
8 B — signalled
✅ Correct
"signalled a new era" — precise formal collocation marking the beginning of a significant period
Strong distractor
sparked off — too informal and implies conflict or controversy rather than organic change
Weak distractor
launched — implies deliberate, organised start rather than an organic historical shift
Obvious distractor
ushered in — overly ceremonial in register; slightly archaic and stilted in this context
Note on item design: Each set of four options follows the Cambridge distractor hierarchy — one correct answer, one strong distractor (genuinely tempting, wrong for a precise collocational or grammatical reason), one weak distractor (related but clearly wrong on reflection), and one obvious distractor (eliminable quickly, though thematically connected). This mirrors authentic CAE item design and allows candidates to work efficiently through elimination rather than having to agonise over every option.
